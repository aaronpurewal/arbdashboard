Section 1

What Is Arbitrage?

Arbitrage happens when two platforms disagree on the odds for the same event. By betting both sides — one on each platform — you lock in a profit no matter who wins.

Think of it like finding the same sneaker priced at $80 on one site and selling for $100 on another. You buy low, sell high, and pocket the difference. In betting, the "price" is the odds.

Example — NBA Moneyline
Platform A
+150
Bet $40.68 on Underdog
Platform B
-140
Bet $59.32 on Favorite
If underdog wins: $40.68 × 2.50 = $101.70 → +$1.70 profit
If favorite wins: $59.32 × 1.71 = $101.69 → +$1.69 profit
Key point: With the right stake sizing, you profit no matter who wins. The scanner calculates the optimal split automatically.

Section 2

What Is +EV (Positive Expected Value)?

Imagine a coin that lands heads 60% of the time, but someone offers you even-money odds (bet $1 to win $1). You'd take that bet every time — not because you'll win every flip, but because over hundreds of flips, you'll come out ahead.

That's positive expected value (+EV). The odds a sportsbook offers are better than the true probability suggests. You can lose any single bet, but over many bets, the math is in your favor.

Expected profit per $100 wagered
Sportsbook Odds
+130
Implied: 43.5%
True Probability
50.0%
From sharp line (devigged)
Expected value per $100 bet: +$15.00 (15% edge)
Arb vs +EV: Arbs guarantee profit on a single event. +EV bets are profitable over many bets, but you can (and will) lose individual ones. The edge compounds over time.

Section 3

What Is Vig? (The Bookmaker's Cut)

If you add up the implied probabilities from a sportsbook's odds, they'll always sum to more than 100%. That extra amount is the vig (short for vigorish) — the bookmaker's built-in profit margin.

Example — A "fair" coin flip

A truly fair coin is 50/50. If odds were fair, you'd pay 50¢ to win $1 on each side.

Fair Price
50¢ / 50¢
Total: 100%
Sportsbook Price
52¢ / 52¢
Total: 104%
The extra 4¢ is the vig: The bookmaker's built-in profit margin

The vig means that even on a 50/50 event, you're paying slightly more than fair price. To find +EV bets, we need to remove the vig and uncover the true probabilities hidden underneath.

Section 4

How We Remove the Vig (Power Devigging)

The simplest way to remove vig is multiplicative devigging — just divide each implied probability by the total so they sum to 100%. But this assumes the bookmaker spreads vig equally across both sides. In reality, they don't.

Bookmakers add more vig to longshots than to favorites. A -500 favorite already has tight margins; there's room to inflate a +400 underdog's price. The Power method models this by finding a correction exponent that shrinks longshot probabilities more than favorite probabilities — matching how bookmakers actually set their lines.

Multiplicative vs Power — Real example

Sportsbook odds: -300 / +250 (implied: 75.0% + 28.6% = 103.6% total)

Method Favorite Underdog Difference
Raw implied 75.0% 28.6% 103.6% total
Multiplicative 72.4% 27.6% Equal vig removed
Power 73.1% 26.9% More vig from longshot

Notice the Power method assigns the underdog a lower true probability (26.9% vs 27.6%) — it attributes more of the vig to the longshot side, which is empirically more accurate.

Show the math

Given implied probabilities p_A and p_B from the odds, we find an exponent k such that:

p_A^k + p_B^k = 1

This is solved numerically (binary search). The devigged probabilities are then:

true_A = p_A^k and true_B = p_B^k

When k < 1, large probabilities (favorites) shrink less than small ones (longshots). This matches empirical data on how books set lines — the vig is disproportionately loaded onto longshots.

The multiplicative method is the special case where k = 1 and you simply normalize: true_A = p_A / (p_A + p_B).

Why it matters: If you use multiplicative devigging, you'll overestimate longshot true probabilities and underestimate their edge — leading to worse bet selection.

Section 5

How We Rank +EV Bets (Kelly Criterion)

Not all +EV bets are equal. A +26% EV longshot that wins 10% of the time sounds amazing, but in practice you'll face long, brutal losing streaks. A +5% EV favorite that wins 65% of the time will grow your bankroll more steadily.

The Kelly criterion answers: "Given my edge and win probability, what fraction of my bankroll should I bet?" It naturally balances edge size against variance — ranking bets by how much they actually grow your bankroll.

Kelly ranking — Longshot vs Favorite
Longshot
+26% EV
Win prob: 10% · Kelly: 1.4%
Favorite
+5% EV
Win prob: 65% · Kelly: 3.8%
Kelly ranks the favorite higher: 3.8% of bankroll vs 1.4%

Despite having a much smaller EV%, the favorite gets a higher Kelly allocation because you'll realize that edge more reliably. The longshot's huge EV% is discounted by its low win rate.

Half-Kelly: We use half-Kelly sizing (divide the Kelly fraction by 2). This gets roughly 75% of the bankroll growth with half the variance. It's the standard recommendation because real-world probability estimates are never perfect.
Show the math

For a bet with decimal odds d and true win probability p:

b = d − 1 (the net payout per unit wagered)

q = 1 − p (probability of losing)

f* = (b × p − q) / b

This gives the fraction of bankroll to wager. Half-Kelly: f*/2.

Example: odds of +200 (decimal 3.0), true prob 40%:

b = 2, p = 0.40, q = 0.60

f* = (2 × 0.40 − 0.60) / 2 = 0.20 / 2 = 10%

Half-Kelly = 5% of bankroll.

Section 6

What the Win Probability Means

Every +EV bet in the scanner shows a win probability derived from the devigged sharp line. The color coding tells you at a glance what kind of bet you're looking at:

● >50% ● 25–50% ● <25%

Green means you'll win more often than not. These bets feel "normal" — you win most of them, and the edge compounds steadily.

Yellow means you're in coin-flip to underdog territory. You'll lose more than half the time, but the payoff when you win compensates.

Gray means you're betting on a longshot. The edge might be real, but expect long losing streaks. A 15% win rate means roughly 1 win per 7 bets — you need a large enough sample size for the edge to show.

Rule of thumb: If you're new to +EV betting, start with green and yellow win rates. Gray longshots have higher variance and require both a larger bankroll and more patience to realize the edge.
